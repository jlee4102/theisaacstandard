## 1:33 — SOUND (90 seconds — your biggest critique, lead with mechanism)

[B-roll: Q20i on a desk; intercut with abstract waveform / EQ-graph visuals]
[On-screen text card: "V-SHAPED TUNING"]

> The biggest issue with the Q20i is the tuning. The sound signature is what audio nerds call a V-shape — elevated bass, scooped mids, lifted treble. Picture a smile when you look at the frequency response graph. The high end is loud. The low end is loud. The middle, where most of the actual music lives, is pulled back.
>
> Why does that matter? The midrange is where vocals live. It's where the body of a guitar lives. It's where snare drums and male voices and most acoustic instruments live. When you scoop that out, everything except the kick drum and the cymbals starts to sound a little distant. A little congested. Like the mix is happening behind a curtain.
>
> Now — bass-forward music actually benefits from this. Hip-hop, EDM, modern pop with synthetic production — these mixes are designed to put the impact in the bass and the energy in the treble. The Q20i flatters them. You'll like it.
>
> But play an acoustic guitar track on the Q20i, or a string quartet, or vocal-forward jazz, and you'll feel the gap immediately. The voices sit behind the kick drum. The piano lacks weight in the lower octaves. It's not broken. It's just tuned for a specific job.
>
> And here's the thing people get wrong: the twenty-two EQ presets in the Soundcore app don't fix this. They shift the balance. They don't change the underlying voicing. You can pull the bass down and lift the mids, and it gets better. But you can't EQ your way to neutral on a headphone that's been engineered for V-shape. The hardware has a character.

## 3:03 — ANC (75 seconds — your strongest finding, this is the sales point)

[B-roll: airport / commuter scene; Q20i in use shot; on-screen text card: "≈ 25 dB ATTENUATION"]

> Here's where the Q20i actually delivers. The noise cancellation is strong for the price.
>
> Anker uses what's called hybrid ANC. There are two microphones on the outside of each earcup that hear ambient noise before it reaches you. Two more microphones on the inside that hear what leaks past the earcup seal. A chip combines both signals and plays back an inverse waveform — literally generating sound waves that cancel the noise.
>
> ANC works best on steady, low-frequency noise. Airplane cabin drone. HVAC hum. Bus and road rumble. The Q20i handles all of those well — measured attenuation lands around 25 decibels in the band the system targets. That's not flagship territory. A Sony XM5 or a Bose QC will out-cancel it, especially in the upper frequencies. But it's the difference between a flight feeling exhausting and feeling manageable. It's the difference between a coffee shop being workable and being too loud.
>
> What ANC doesn't do — on any headphone, not just the Q20i — is block transient noise. People talking. Dishes clattering. Sirens. Those are too irregular for the algorithm to predict and cancel. So if you're buying these expecting them to silence your noisy coworker, that's not how the technology works.
>
> For the price, the ANC alone is a legitimate reason to buy these.

> Is that a notable gap from the spec sheet? Yes. Is it still excellent for the category? Also yes. Forty-nine hours is two and a half long-haul flights between charges. The five-minute fast-charge is real too — you get about four hours of playback from five minutes plugged in. Which is the number that actually matters if you forget to charge your headphones, which I do, constantly.

## 5:44 — THE THREE THINGS NOBODY MENTIONS (90 seconds — your real value-add)

[On-screen text card: "THREE THINGS NOBODY MENTIONS"]
[Each subsection: dedicated text overlay card]

> Now the part marketing doesn't cover.
>
> **Number one. The three-point-five millimeter cable disables ANC.** Plug it in, and the noise cancellation turns off completely. This matters because the most useful in-flight setup is plugging into the seat-back entertainment system while keeping ANC on for the cabin noise. The Q20i can't do that. Most flagships can. It's a real functional gap that the spec sheet doesn't make obvious.
>
> **Number two. The microphone falls apart in noise.** In a quiet room, it's fine. On a phone call from your couch, fine. The moment there's ambient noise — a café, an open office, walking down a city street — the noise suppression algorithm can't isolate your voice cleanly, and your callers will hear it. If you take meetings from anywhere that isn't a closet, this is a real limitation.
>
> **Number three. Bluetooth 5.0, not 5.3.** The Q20i ships with the older Bluetooth protocol. Connection is stable in daily use. But it's less power-efficient and shorter range than the current generation, and it's a reminder that you're shopping in a budget tier. The newer Anker P20i earbuds, half the price, ship with 5.3. The Q20i didn't get the upgrade.
